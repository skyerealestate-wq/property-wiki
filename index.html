üìã Overview

This comprehensive guide covers all stages of tenant onboarding from initial application through to successful move-in. Following these procedures ensures legal compliance and sets the foundation for a positive tenancy.

1. Tenant Referencing

Purpose: Verify tenant suitability and ability to pay rent

Information to Collect

  • Personal Details: Full name, date of birth, current address (3 years history)
  • Employment: Employer name, position, salary, length of employment
  • Financial: Bank statements (3 months), proof of income
  • Previous Tenancies: Landlord references (2 years)
  • Identification: Passport or driving license, proof of address

Reference Checks to Conduct

  • Credit check: Use Experian, Equifax or specialist service (e.g., Homelet, Rent4Sure)
  • Employer reference: Verify employment and salary
  • Previous landlord reference: Confirm rent payment history and property care
  • Affordability assessment: Rent should be ‚â§30% of gross monthly income

Red Flags

  • CCJs, defaults, or bankruptcies on credit file
  • Rent arrears with previous landlord
  • Income insufficient to cover rent (consider guarantor)
  • Gaps in employment or rental history
  • False or misleading information provided
‚ö†Ô∏è GDPR Compliance

Obtain written consent before conducting credit checks. Store data securely and delete after 6 months if application unsuccessful.

2. Right to Rent Checks

Legal Requirement: Must be completed before tenancy starts

When to Conduct

Before the tenancy agreement is signed and the tenant moves in. Landlords face civil penalties up to ¬£3,000 per occupant for failure to conduct checks.

Acceptable Documents (Group A - No Follow-up Required)

  • UK or Irish passport (current or expired within last 2 years)
  • Current EU/EEA passport with settled/pre-settled status
  • Current Biometric Residence Permit (BRP)
  • Current passport with valid UK visa

Acceptable Documents (Group B - Two Required)

  • Birth certificate + official document with NI number
  • UK driving license (photocard) + official document with NI number
  • Bank statement + utility bill (each from different source)

Verification Process

  1. Check documents in person (or via certified ID verification service)
  2. Verify they appear genuine and haven't been altered
  3. Check the photo matches the tenant
  4. Copy documents and record the date of the check
  5. Store copies securely for duration of tenancy + 1 year

For Non-UK/Irish Nationals

  • Check immigration status using Home Office online service
  • Keep evidence of online check (share code provided by tenant)
  • May require follow-up checks every 12 months depending on visa status

3. AST Preparation

Assured Shorthold Tenancy Agreement

Key Clauses to Include

  • Parties: Full names and addresses of landlord and all tenants
  • Property address: Complete address including postcode
  • Term: Start date, initial fixed period (typically 6 or 12 months)
  • Rent: Amount, due date, payment method, review terms
  • Deposit: Amount and protection scheme details
  • Permitted occupiers: All adults living in the property
  • Utilities: Who pays for what (usually tenant pays all)
  • Maintenance: Landlord and tenant responsibilities
  • Access: 24 hours notice for inspections
  • Break clauses: If applicable (typically after 6 months)
  • Pets: Permitted or prohibited (cannot blanket ban)
  • Smoking: Policy on smoking in the property

Required Additions Under Tenant Fees Act 2019

  • Permitted payments: rent, deposit, holding deposit
  • Default fees: late payment (max 3% above BoE base rate), lost keys (actual cost)
  • Early termination fees (if break clause exists)

Recommended Template Sources

  • NRLA (National Residential Landlords Association) AST template
  • Shelter / Citizens Advice approved templates
  • Legal review recommended for non-standard clauses
‚ö†Ô∏è Unfair Terms

Avoid clauses that are one-sided or could be deemed unfair under Consumer Rights Act 2015. Examples: charging for fair wear and tear, restricting reasonable guests, requiring professional cleaning.

5. Deposit Protection

Legal Requirement: Within 30 days of receipt

Choose a Scheme

Government-approved schemes:

  • DPS (Deposit Protection Service): Free custodial option available
  • MyDeposits: Popular with agents, good online portal
  • TDS (Tenancy Deposit Scheme): Long-established, reliable

Protection Options

Type Custodial Insurance-Based
Who holds deposit Scheme holds it You hold it
Cost Free ~¬£25-50 per tenancy
Interest Tenant gets interest You get interest
Best for Single landlords Agencies, portfolio landlords

Protection Process

  1. Receive deposit: Ideally by bank transfer for clear audit trail
  2. Protect within 30 days: Register with chosen scheme
  3. Provide Prescribed Information: Must give tenant within 30 days
  4. Keep records: Save protection certificate and confirmation of tenant receipt

Prescribed Information Must Include

  • Deposit amount and date received
  • Address of let property
  • Which scheme protects the deposit
  • Contact details for scheme
  • Landlord and tenant names/addresses
  • How to apply for deposit return
  • Information about Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)
  • What to do if you can't agree on deductions
‚ö†Ô∏è Penalties for Non-Compliance

Failure to protect deposit correctly can result in paying tenant 1-3x the deposit amount. You also cannot serve a Section 21 notice until this is remedied.

Walk-Through Checklist

  2. Review inventory together - both sign and date
  3. Demonstrate appliances: Boiler, washing machine, shower, etc.
  4. Show emergency controls: Stopcock, consumer unit, gas valve

  6. Review bin collection days and recycling rules
  7. Answer questions about the property or area
  8. Take meter readings together and photograph
  9. Hand over keys and get signed receipt
  10. Set expectations: Communication channels, reporting repairs, inspections

First Contact Timeline

  • Day 1-3: Send welcome message, confirm move-in went smoothly
  • Week 1: Quick check-in on any questions or issues
  • Month 1: First informal inspection (if tenant agrees)
  • Month 3: First formal quarterly inspection

Communication Expectations

Set clear guidelines:

  • Routine queries: Email, response within 24-48 hours
  • Maintenance issues: Online portal or WhatsApp, response within 24 hours
  • Emergencies: Phone number, response immediately
  • Quarterly inspections: 24 hours written notice required

Rent Arrears Process

Section 8 Notice (Arrears)

Eviction for rent arrears

When to Use Section 8

  • Mandatory ground: 2+ months arrears at both date of notice AND date of hearing
  • Discretionary grounds: Any arrears (judge decides if reasonable)
  • Can be used during fixed term (unlike Section 21)
  • Can claim arrears in same court action

Section 8 Process

  1. Serve Section 8 Notice: Minimum 2 weeks notice (or 2 months if using mandatory ground)
  2. Wait for notice period: Tenant may leave or pay arrears
  3. Apply to court: If tenant still present after notice expires
  4. Court hearing: Usually 4-8 weeks after application
  5. Possession order: If granted, usually 14-28 days for tenant to leave
  6. Bailiff eviction: If tenant doesn't leave voluntarily

Notice Requirements

  • Use Form 3 (Section 8 Notice)
  • Specify ground(s) for possession - typically Ground 8 (2+ months arrears) or Ground 10/11 (some arrears)
  • Provide full particulars of arrears
  • Serve by hand delivery, first class post, or recorded delivery
  • Allow 2 working days for postal service

Court Application (Form N5)

  • Complete form N5 (Claim for Possession)
  • Attach copy of tenancy agreement
  • Attach copy of Section 8 notice
  • Attach schedule of arrears
  • Fee: Currently ¬£355 (check gov.uk for current fees)
  • Submit to local county court

Section 21 Notice (No-Fault)

Alternative route for eviction

When to Consider Section 21

  • Persistent small arrears (under 2 months)
  • Want guaranteed possession (no judge discretion)
  • Multiple issues beyond arrears
  • Can only serve after fixed term ends (or if periodic tenancy)

Section 21 Requirements

  • 2 months notice (must end on last day of rental period)
  • Can't serve in first 4 months of tenancy
  • Must have provided: How to Rent Guide, Gas Safety Certificate, EPC, deposit protected correctly
  • Deposit prescribed information given within 30 days
  • No outstanding enforcement notices on property
üìã Section 8 vs Section 21

Section 8: Faster if 2+ months arrears (mandatory ground), can claim money owed, but requires court hearing and judge approval.

Section 21: Guaranteed possession if valid, but longer notice period, can't use if you've failed compliance, can't claim arrears in same action.

What you can and cannot charge for

Fair Wear and Tear (Cannot Charge)

Reasonable deterioration from normal use over time:

  • Minor scuff marks on walls from normal use
  • Light carpet wear in high-traffic areas
  • Fading of paint or wallpaper due to sunlight
  • Light scratches on worktops from normal kitchen use
  • Minor dulling of wood floors
  • Slight loosening of door handles from use
  • General aging of seals and grouting
  • Dust and light dirt accumulated over time

Tenant Damage (Can Charge)

Damage beyond normal use, neglect, or failure to maintain:

  • Holes in walls (except small picture hooks)
  • Burns or large stains on carpets
  • Broken tiles, damaged worktops
  • Crayon/pen marks on walls
  • Broken windows or doors
  • Damaged appliances from misuse
  • Pet damage (scratches, urine stains)
  • Damage from unapproved alterations
  • Excessive mould from lack of ventilation
  • Garden neglect beyond overgrowth

Cleaning Issues (Can Charge)

  • Excessively dirty oven/hob
  • Bathroom not cleaned (toilet, bath, tiles)
  • Kitchen surfaces covered in grease
  • Carpets needing professional clean due to stains
  • Windows not cleaned
  • Rubbish left in property
  • Items abandoned in garden/shed

Issue: Floor joists rot if sub-floor area damp/poorly ventilated

  • Prevention: Keep airbricks clear at all times (6-8 per property usually)
  • Prevention: Don't allow soil/paving to rise above airbrick level
  • Prevention: Check sub-floor every 2-3 years for dampness
  • Warning signs: Bouncy floors, musty smell, visible rot on skirting
  • Typical cost: Joist repair ¬£500-1,500 per joist

3. Cast Iron Drainage

Issue: Original drains crack/corrode after 100+ years

  • Prevention: Annual drain inspection (CCTV every 3-5 years)
  • Prevention: Rodding points kept accessible
  • Prevention: Don't plant trees near drains (roots seek water)
  • Warning signs: Slow-draining sinks, bad smells, damp patches in yard
  • Typical cost: Drain lining ¬£800-1,500, full replacement ¬£2,000-5,000

4. Sash Window Deterioration

Issue: Original timber windows suffer from rot, broken cords

  • Prevention: External paint every 5-7 years
  • Prevention: Replace putty as it fails
  • Prevention: Fix broken sash cords promptly (prevent further damage)
  • Draught sealing: Install brush seals to improve efficiency
  • Typical cost: Sash cord replacement ¬£80-150 per window, full refurb ¬£800-1,200 per window

5. Roof Slate Issues

Issue: 100-year-old slates become brittle, nails corrode

  • Prevention: Annual visual inspection from ground
  • Prevention: Check after every storm
  • Prevention: Fix displaced slates immediately (rain enters quickly)
  • Warning signs: Slates in yard, water stains on ceiling, drips in loft
  • Typical cost: Individual slate ¬£80-150, full re-roof ¬£8,000-15,000

6. Chimney Problems

Issue: Unused chimneys deteriorate, pots become unstable

  • Prevention: Cap unused chimneys (but maintain ventilation)
  • Prevention: Check flashing where chimney meets roof
  • Prevention: Repoint chimney stack every 20-30 years
  • Warning signs: Damp patches around chimney breast, loose pots visible
  • Typical cost: Repointing ¬£600-1,200, removal ¬£1,000-2,500

7. Single-Skin Rear Extension Damp

Issue: Many terraces have thin brick rear extensions that get cold/damp

  • Prevention: Keep pointing perfect on these walls
  • Prevention: Consider internal insulation if chronic problem
  • Prevention: Ensure good ventilation to prevent condensation
  • Heating: Keep these rooms adequately heated in winter
  • Typical cost: Internal insulation ¬£800-1,500 per room

8. Party Wall Water Ingress

Issue: Rain enters through party wall (especially gable ends)

  • Check: Top of party wall pointing and coping stones
  • Coordination: May need to coordinate with neighbour for repairs
  • Legal: Costs typically split 50/50 under Party Wall Act
  • Typical cost: Gable wall repointing ¬£1,000-2,000 (split with neighbour)

Legal Notices - Possession

Section 8 and Section 21 notices
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Legal Documents

These notices must be completed with extreme care. Any errors can invalidate the notice and delay possession by months. Consider seeking legal advice, especially for your first time serving notice.

Section 8 Notice (Form 3)

When to use: Seeking possession for rent arrears or other breach

Grounds:

  • Ground 8: 2+ months rent arrears (mandatory ground - judge must grant possession if arrears still 2+ months at hearing)
  • Ground 10: Some rent arrears (discretionary)
  • Ground 11: Persistent late payment (discretionary)

Notice period:

  • Ground 8 (mandatory): Minimum 2 weeks
  • Grounds 10/11 (discretionary): Minimum 2 months

Critical requirements:

  • Use correct form (government Form 3)
  • Specify exact grounds being used
  • Provide full particulars of arrears with dates and amounts
  • State correct notice period date
  • Serve correctly (hand delivery or first class post)
  • Keep proof of service

What happens next:

  • After notice period expires, can apply to court
  • Court hearing typically 4-8 weeks after application
  • Can claim arrears in same proceedings
  • If successful, judge grants possession order

Download: Form-Section-8-Notice.docx

Section 21 Notice (Form 6A)

When to use: No-fault eviction (assured shorthold tenancy)

Advantages:

  • Don't need to prove grounds
  • Judge must grant possession if notice valid
  • Tenant can't defend on grounds of arrears disputes

Critical requirements before serving:

  • Can't serve in first 4 months of tenancy
  • Must have given tenant: How to Rent Guide, Gas Safety Cert, EPC
  • Deposit protected correctly with Prescribed Information given
  • No outstanding improvement or prohibition notices
  • If fixed term: can only serve after fixed term ends

Notice period:

  • Minimum 2 months
  • Must end on last day of rental period (e.g. if rent due 1st of month, notice must expire on last day of month)
  • Notice valid for 6 months - must apply to court within this time

What happens next:

  • After notice expires, can apply for possession
  • Court grants possession without hearing if all correct (accelerated procedure)
  • Cannot claim rent arrears in Section 21 proceedings (need separate claim)

Common mistakes that invalidate Section 21:

  • Serving in first 4 months
  • Not providing How to Rent guide
  • Deposit not protected within 30 days
  • Wrong end date (not last day of period)
  • Less than 2 months notice

Best Practices for Property Inspections

How to conduct effective inspections

Before the Inspection

  • Give proper notice: 24 hours minimum in writing (see Letter Templates for notice template)
  • Choose reasonable time: 10am-5pm on weekday, avoid early mornings/late evenings
  • Confirm appointment: Follow up with tenant 1-2 days before
  • Prepare checklist: Print relevant checklist and bring clipboard
  • Charge devices: Phone/camera for photos, ensure good battery life
  • Bring previous inspection: Move-in inventory or last quarterly report for comparison
  • Have contact details: Plumber, electrician numbers in case urgent issues found

During the Inspection

  • Be professional: Polite, friendly but thorough
  • Allow sufficient time: Don't rush - 45-90 minutes depending on property size
  • Work systematically: Follow checklist room by room
  • Take comprehensive photos: Wide shots + close-ups of any issues
  • Open cupboards/look under sinks: Don't just check visible areas
  • Test everything: Smoke alarms, heating, water pressure, windows, doors
  • Note specific locations: "Damp patch, bedroom 2, external wall, top left corner" not just "some damp"
  • Be fair: Distinguish fair wear from damage
  • Engage tenant: If present, discuss any concerns they have
  • Check airbricks: Critical for old properties - ensure all visible and clear

After the Inspection

  • Complete checklist immediately: While fresh in mind
  • Organize photos: Clear file naming (e.g., "22-Bedroom1-DampPatch.jpg")
  • Follow up on issues: Contact contractors for quotes/repairs
  • Thank tenant: Quick email thanking them for access
  • If issues found: Inform tenant what will be fixed and timeline
  • Store records: Keep inspection reports for full tenancy + 6 years after
  • Update maintenance log: Track recurring issues
  • Schedule next inspection: Set reminder for 3 months time

Specific Checks for Victorian/Edwardian Terraces

Focus areas for old properties

Every Inspection Should Include:

  • Damp checks: External walls, corners, window reveals, chimney breasts
  • Airbrick inspection: All airbricks visible and unobstructed (sub-floor ventilation critical)

  • Heating: Boiler working, radiators warm throughout property

  • Evidence value: Proper inspections critical for deposit disputes and possession claims
