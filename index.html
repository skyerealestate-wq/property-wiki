üìã Overview

This comprehensive guide covers all stages of tenant onboarding from initial application through to successful move-in. Following these procedures ensures legal compliance and sets the foundation for a positive tenancy.

1. Tenant Referencing

Purpose: Verify tenant suitability and ability to pay rent

Information to Collect

  • Personal Details: Full name, date of birth, current address (3 years history)
  • Employment: Employer name, position, salary, length of employment
  • Financial: Bank statements (3 months), proof of income
  • Previous Tenancies: Landlord references (2 years)
  • Identification: Passport or driving license, proof of address

Reference Checks to Conduct

  • Credit check: Use Experian, Equifax or specialist service (e.g., Homelet, Rent4Sure)
  • Employer reference: Verify employment and salary
  • Previous landlord reference: Confirm rent payment history and property care
  • Affordability assessment: Rent should be ‚â§30% of gross monthly income

Red Flags

  • CCJs, defaults, or bankruptcies on credit file
  • Rent arrears with previous landlord
  • Income insufficient to cover rent (consider guarantor)
  • Gaps in employment or rental history
  • False or misleading information provided
‚ö†Ô∏è GDPR Compliance

Obtain written consent before conducting credit checks. Store data securely and delete after 6 months if application unsuccessful.

2. Right to Rent Checks

Legal Requirement: Must be completed before tenancy starts

When to Conduct

Before the tenancy agreement is signed and the tenant moves in. Landlords face civil penalties up to ¬£3,000 per occupant for failure to conduct checks.

Acceptable Documents (Group A - No Follow-up Required)

  • UK or Irish passport (current or expired within last 2 years)
  • Current EU/EEA passport with settled/pre-settled status
  • Current Biometric Residence Permit (BRP)
  • Current passport with valid UK visa

Acceptable Documents (Group B - Two Required)

  • Birth certificate + official document with NI number
  • UK driving license (photocard) + official document with NI number
  • Bank statement + utility bill (each from different source)

Verification Process

  1. Check documents in person (or via certified ID verification service)
  2. Verify they appear genuine and haven't been altered
  3. Check the photo matches the tenant
  4. Copy documents and record the date of the check
  5. Store copies securely for duration of tenancy + 1 year

For Non-UK/Irish Nationals

  • Check immigration status using Home Office online service
  • Keep evidence of online check (share code provided by tenant)
  • May require follow-up checks every 12 months depending on visa status

3. AST Preparation

Assured Shorthold Tenancy Agreement

Key Clauses to Include

  • Parties: Full names and addresses of landlord and all tenants
  • Property address: Complete address including postcode
  • Term: Start date, initial fixed period (typically 6 or 12 months)
  • Rent: Amount, due date, payment method, review terms
  • Deposit: Amount and protection scheme details
  • Permitted occupiers: All adults living in the property
  • Utilities: Who pays for what (usually tenant pays all)
  • Maintenance: Landlord and tenant responsibilities
  • Access: 24 hours notice for inspections
  • Break clauses: If applicable (typically after 6 months)
  • Pets: Permitted or prohibited (cannot blanket ban)
  • Smoking: Policy on smoking in the property

Required Additions Under Tenant Fees Act 2019

  • Permitted payments: rent, deposit, holding deposit
  • Default fees: late payment (max 3% above BoE base rate), lost keys (actual cost)
  • Early termination fees (if break clause exists)

Recommended Template Sources

  • NRLA (National Residential Landlords Association) AST template
  • Shelter / Citizens Advice approved templates
  • Legal review recommended for non-standard clauses
‚ö†Ô∏è Unfair Terms

Avoid clauses that are one-sided or could be deemed unfair under Consumer Rights Act 2015. Examples: charging for fair wear and tear, restricting reasonable guests, requiring professional cleaning.

Rent Arrears Process

Escalation procedure for late rent payments.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Principle

Act quickly but fairly. Early intervention prevents small issues becoming major problems. Always document everything and maintain professionalism throughout.

Prevention Strategies

Stop arrears before they start
  • Clear payment terms: Rent due date, amount, and payment method in AST
  • Standing orders: Encourage tenants to set up automatic payments
  • Payment reminders: Send friendly reminder 2-3 days before due date
  • Early communication: Check in if payment is 1 day late
  • Good relationships: Tenants who feel valued are more likely to communicate issues
  • Reference thoroughly: Proper tenant vetting reduces arrears risk

Escalation Timeline

From friendly reminder to legal action

Day 1: Rent Due Date

  • Rent should be received by this date (per tenancy agreement)
  • No action needed if payment received

Day 2: Initial Contact (Informal)

Action: Quick, friendly check-in

  • Send text message or WhatsApp: "Hi [Name], just checking the rent payment - it doesn't seem to have arrived yet. Can you confirm when it'll be sent? Thanks!"
  • Tone: Assume honest mistake, give benefit of doubt
  • Record: Note in tenant file that contact was made

Day 5: First Formal Reminder

Action: Send formal reminder letter/email

  • Use Rent Reminder Letter Template (see Letter Templates page)
  • State amount owed, due date, and request immediate payment
  • Ask tenant to contact you to discuss if experiencing difficulties
  • Send by email AND post (or hand deliver)
  • Record: Keep copy and proof of sending

Day 7: Phone Call

Action: Direct conversation

  • Call tenant to discuss arrears
  • Ask if there are temporary difficulties
  • Offer payment plan if tenant is cooperative (see Payment Plans below)
  • Explain consequences of non-payment

Section 8 Notice (Arrears)

Eviction for rent arrears

When to Use Section 8

  • Mandatory ground: 2+ months arrears at both date of notice AND date of hearing
  • Discretionary grounds: Any arrears (judge decides if reasonable)
  • Can be used during fixed term (unlike Section 21)
  • Can claim arrears in same court action

Section 8 Process

  1. Serve Section 8 Notice: Minimum 2 weeks notice (or 2 months if using mandatory ground)
  2. Wait for notice period: Tenant may leave or pay arrears
  3. Apply to court: If tenant still present after notice expires
  4. Court hearing: Usually 4-8 weeks after application
  5. Possession order: If granted, usually 14-28 days for tenant to leave
  6. Bailiff eviction: If tenant doesn't leave voluntarily

Notice Requirements

  • Use Form 3 (Section 8 Notice)
  • Specify ground(s) for possession - typically Ground 8 (2+ months arrears) or Ground 10/11 (some arrears)
  • Provide full particulars of arrears
  • Serve by hand delivery, first class post, or recorded delivery
  • Allow 2 working days for postal service

Court Application (Form N5)

  • Complete form N5 (Claim for Possession)
  • Attach copy of tenancy agreement
  • Attach copy of Section 8 notice
  • Attach schedule of arrears
  • Fee: Currently ¬£355 (check gov.uk for current fees)
  • Submit to local county court

Section 21 Notice (No-Fault)

Alternative route for eviction

When to Consider Section 21

  • Persistent small arrears (under 2 months)
  • Want guaranteed possession (no judge discretion)
  • Multiple issues beyond arrears
  • Can only serve after fixed term ends (or if periodic tenancy)

Section 21 Requirements

  • 2 months notice (must end on last day of rental period)
  • Can't serve in first 4 months of tenancy
  • Must have provided: How to Rent Guide, Gas Safety Certificate, EPC, deposit protected correctly
  • Deposit prescribed information given within 30 days
  • No outstanding enforcement notices on property
üìã Section 8 vs Section 21

Section 8: Faster if 2+ months arrears (mandatory ground), can claim money owed, but requires court hearing and judge approval.

Section 21: Guaranteed possession if valid, but longer notice period, can't use if you've failed compliance, can't claim arrears in same action.

Fair Wear and Tear vs Damage

What you can and cannot charge for

Fair Wear and Tear (Cannot Charge)

Reasonable deterioration from normal use over time:

  • Minor scuff marks on walls from normal use
  • Light carpet wear in high-traffic areas
  • Fading of paint or wallpaper due to sunlight
  • Light scratches on worktops from normal kitchen use
  • Minor dulling of wood floors
  • Slight loosening of door handles from use
  • General aging of seals and grouting
  • Dust and light dirt accumulated over time

Tenant Damage (Can Charge)

Damage beyond normal use, neglect, or failure to maintain:

  • Holes in walls (except small picture hooks)
  • Burns or large stains on carpets
  • Broken tiles, damaged worktops
  • Crayon/pen marks on walls
  • Broken windows or doors
  • Damaged appliances from misuse
  • Pet damage (scratches, urine stains)
  • Damage from unapproved alterations
  • Excessive mould from lack of ventilation
  • Garden neglect beyond overgrowth

Cleaning Issues (Can Charge)

  • Excessively dirty oven/hob
  • Bathroom not cleaned (toilet, bath, tiles)
  • Kitchen surfaces covered in grease
  • Carpets needing professional clean due to stains
  • Windows not cleaned
  • Rubbish left in property
  • Items abandoned in garden/shed
‚ö†Ô∏è Betterment

You cannot charge for "betterment" - making the property better than when tenant moved in. Example: if tenant damages 5-year-old carpet, you can only charge depreciated value, not cost of brand new carpet. TDS typically uses 5-10 year lifespan for carpets/appliances.

Property-Specific Issues: Victorian/Edwardian Terraces

Common problems and prevention

1. Solid Wall Dampness

Issue: No cavity wall = rain penetrates during storms

  • Prevention: Keep pointing in good condition (repoint every 30-50 years minimum)
  • Prevention: Maintain external paint on render/brickwork
  • Prevention: Fix any cracked render immediately
  • Check: After heavy rain, check internal walls for damp patches
  • Typical cost: Repointing front/back ¬£1,500-3,000

2. Suspended Timber Floor Issues

Issue: Floor joists rot if sub-floor area damp/poorly ventilated

  • Prevention: Keep airbricks clear at all times (6-8 per property usually)
  • Prevention: Don't allow soil/paving to rise above airbrick level
  • Prevention: Check sub-floor every 2-3 years for dampness
  • Warning signs: Bouncy floors, musty smell, visible rot on skirting
  • Typical cost: Joist repair ¬£500-1,500 per joist

3. Cast Iron Drainage

Issue: Original drains crack/corrode after 100+ years

  • Prevention: Annual drain inspection (CCTV every 3-5 years)
  • Prevention: Rodding points kept accessible
  • Prevention: Don't plant trees near drains (roots seek water)
  • Warning signs: Slow-draining sinks, bad smells, damp patches in yard
  • Typical cost: Drain lining ¬£800-1,500, full replacement ¬£2,000-5,000

4. Sash Window Deterioration

Issue: Original timber windows suffer from rot, broken cords

  • Prevention: External paint every 5-7 years
  • Prevention: Replace putty as it fails
  • Prevention: Fix broken sash cords promptly (prevent further damage)
  • Draught sealing: Install brush seals to improve efficiency
  • Typical cost: Sash cord replacement ¬£80-150 per window, full refurb ¬£800-1,200 per window

5. Roof Slate Issues

Issue: 100-year-old slates become brittle, nails corrode

  • Prevention: Annual visual inspection from ground
  • Prevention: Check after every storm
  • Prevention: Fix displaced slates immediately (rain enters quickly)
  • Warning signs: Slates in yard, water stains on ceiling, drips in loft
  • Typical cost: Individual slate ¬£80-150, full re-roof ¬£8,000-15,000

6. Chimney Problems

Issue: Unused chimneys deteriorate, pots become unstable

  • Prevention: Cap unused chimneys (but maintain ventilation)
  • Prevention: Check flashing where chimney meets roof
  • Prevention: Repoint chimney stack every 20-30 years
  • Warning signs: Damp patches around chimney breast, loose pots visible
  • Typical cost: Repointing ¬£600-1,200, removal ¬£1,000-2,500

7. Single-Skin Rear Extension Damp

Issue: Many terraces have thin brick rear extensions that get cold/damp

  • Prevention: Keep pointing perfect on these walls
  • Prevention: Consider internal insulation if chronic problem
  • Prevention: Ensure good ventilation to prevent condensation
  • Heating: Keep these rooms adequately heated in winter
  • Typical cost: Internal insulation ¬£800-1,500 per room

8. Party Wall Water Ingress

Issue: Rain enters through party wall (especially gable ends)

  • Check: Top of party wall pointing and coping stones
  • Coordination: May need to coordinate with neighbour for repairs
  • Legal: Costs typically split 50/50 under Party Wall Act
  • Typical cost: Gable wall repointing ¬£1,000-2,000 (split with neighbour)

Right to Rent Checks

Legal requirement to verify tenant's right to rent in the UK.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Legal Requirement

Failure to conduct Right to Rent checks can result in civil penalties up to ¬£3,000 per illegal occupant, and potential criminal prosecution for repeat or knowing breaches. You must check EVERY adult (18+) occupying the property.

When to Conduct Checks

  • Before tenancy starts: Must complete checks before tenant moves in or signs tenancy agreement
  • Every adult occupant: All adults living in property (including permitted occupiers)
  • Before offering tenancy: Best practice - check at referencing stage
  • Follow-up checks: Required for some visa holders (see below)

Acceptable Documents

List A - No Follow-Up Required (Unlimited Right to Rent)

One document from this list provides unlimited right to rent:

  • British or Irish passport (current or expired within last 2 years)
  • EU/EEA/Swiss passport with UK settled or pre-settled status
  • Current Biometric Residence Permit (BRP) showing indefinite leave
  • Current passport with endorsed stamp showing indefinite leave to remain
  • Certificate of registration or naturalisation as British citizen
  • Current Immigration Status Document with indefinite leave to remain
  • Birth or adoption certificate + official document with National Insurance number (e.g., P45, P60, payslip)
List B - Follow-Up May Be Required (Time-Limited Right)

Documents showing time-limited right to rent (may need follow-up check before expiry):

  • Current passport with valid UK visa
  • Current Biometric Residence Permit showing limited leave to remain
  • Current Immigration Status Document with limited leave to remain
  • Certificate of Application (less than 6 months old) + positive verification from Home Office
List B Alternative (Two Documents Required)

If no passport/BRP, two documents from different sources:

  • Birth certificate + official document with NI number from different issuer
  • UK driving licence (photocard or old-style paper) + official document with NI number
  • Letter from employer + bank statement (each must be from different source and dated within 3 months)

Verification Process

Step-by-Step Checks
  1. Check documents in original: Must see physical documents, not copies (unless using certified ID service)
  2. Check in presence of tenant: Ideally face-to-face, or via approved video call
  3. Verify document authenticity:
    • Document appears genuine (not forged/tampered)
    • Photos match the person
    • Dates of birth consistent across documents
    • Document in date (or expired less than 2 years for passports)
  4. Check immigration status online (if applicable): Use Home Office right to rent checking service with tenant's share code
  5. Make clear copies: Copy entire document (both sides if applicable)
  6. Record the check date: Write date on copies
  7. Store securely: Keep for duration of tenancy + 1 year minimum

Online Right to Rent Checks

Home Office Online Service

For: EEA nationals, non-EEA nationals with biometric residence cards/permits

Process:

  1. Tenant generates a share code at: www.gov.uk/prove-right-to-rent
  2. Tenant provides you with: share code and date of birth
  3. You check online using the share code within specified timeframe
  4. System confirms right to rent status
  5. Print/save the confirmation as evidence
  6. Keep confirmation with your records

Follow-Up Checks Required If:

  • Document shows leave to remain expires before end of tenancy
  • Certificate of Application shown (follow up when decision made)
  • Status shows time-limited permission

Timing: Conduct follow-up check before permission expires or when notified by Home Office

What to Look For (Red Flags)

  • Document issues: Poor quality photos, alterations, mismatched details, wrong format for year issued
  • Photo mismatch: Person doesn't match photo (consider age, but significant differences are suspicious)
  • Inconsistent information: Name/DOB differs between documents
  • Expired documents: Passport expired more than 2 years ago, visa/BRP expired
  • Tenant reluctance: Unwilling to provide documents, provides only copies, says documents are "being renewed"
  • Suspicious behaviour: Multiple people claiming to live at address who don't appear on checks

If concerned: Do not proceed with tenancy. You cannot be penalised for refusing to rent if you have genuine concerns about document validity.

Statutory Excuse (Your Legal Protection)

If you follow the correct procedure, you establish a "statutory excuse" which protects you from penalties even if the tenant later turns out to have no right to rent.

Statutory Excuse is Established When:

  • You checked original documents in person (or via approved method)
  • Documents appeared genuine and belonged to the person
  • Documents showed right to rent at the time of check
  • You made clear copies
  • You kept copies securely
  • You conducted follow-up checks when required

Statutory Excuse is Lost If:

  • You are notified by Home Office that tenant has no right to rent
  • You continue to rent to them after notification
  • You had reasonable cause to believe documents were false
  • You didn't conduct required follow-up checks

When to use: Seeking possession for rent arrears or other breach

  • Ground 10: Some rent arrears (discretionary)
  • Ground 11: Persistent late payment (discretionary)

Notice period:

  • Ground 8 (mandatory): Minimum 2 weeks
  • Grounds 10/11 (discretionary): Minimum 2 months

Critical requirements:

  • Use correct form (government Form 3)
  • Specify exact grounds being used
  • Provide full particulars of arrears with dates and amounts
  • State correct notice period date
  • Serve correctly (hand delivery or first class post)
  • Keep proof of service

What happens next:

  • After notice period expires, can apply to court
  • Court hearing typically 4-8 weeks after application
  • Can claim arrears in same proceedings
  • If successful, judge grants possession order

Section 21 Notice (Form 6A)

When to use: No-fault eviction (assured shorthold tenancy)

Advantages:

  • Don't need to prove grounds
  • Judge must grant possession if notice valid
  • Tenant can't defend on grounds of arrears disputes

  • Can't serve in first 4 months of tenancy

Smoke & Carbon Monoxide Alarms

Legal requirements for fire and CO detection in rental properties.

üö® Life Safety Critical

Smoke and CO alarms save lives. Penalties up to ¬£5,000 for non-compliance, but more importantly, failure can result in tenant deaths and potential manslaughter charges.

Legal Requirements

Smoke Alarms (Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Alarm (England) Regulations 2015)
  • Where: At least one smoke alarm on every storey of the property used as living accommodation
  • Living accommodation: Includes bedrooms, living rooms, kitchens, bathrooms - NOT just bedrooms
  • Type: Mains-powered with battery backup recommended (can be battery-only but must be tamper-proof)
  • Testing: Must test on day tenant moves in
  • Maintenance: Landlord responsible for ensuring working order
Carbon Monoxide Alarms
  • Where: In any room with a fixed combustion appliance (except gas cookers)
  • Combustion appliances: Boilers, fires (gas/solid fuel), wood burners
  • Not required for: Gas cookers/hobs (but recommended in kitchen with gas hob)
  • Type: Must meet British Standard EN 50291
  • Testing: Must test on day tenant moves in

Recommended Setup for Terraced Houses

Typical 2-bed terraced house:

  • Ground floor: Smoke alarm in hallway + CO alarm near boiler (if ground floor boiler)
  • First floor: Smoke alarm on landing (serves bedroom floor)
  • Kitchen: Heat alarm recommended (not smoke - triggers from cooking), or optical smoke alarm
  • Living room with fireplace: CO alarm if fireplace in use

Alarm Type Guidance:

  • Ionization alarms: Best for fast-flaming fires, can trigger from cooking
  • Optical/photoelectric alarms: Best for slow-smoldering fires, less false alarms from cooking
  • Heat alarms: For kitchens - detects temperature rise, no false alarms from steam/cooking
  • Combined alarms: Smoke + CO in single unit (convenient for rental properties)

Installation Guidelines

Positioning Smoke Alarms:

  • Mount on ceiling in center of room/landing
  • At least 300mm from walls and light fittings
  • Not in corners where air circulation is poor
  • Away from bathrooms/kitchens where steam triggers false alarms
  • Not near doors or windows where drafts affect performance

Positioning CO Alarms:

  • 1-3 meters from combustion appliance
  • At head height (CO is similar weight to air)
  • NOT on ceiling (unlike smoke alarms)
  • NOT directly above appliance
  • NOT in damp or dusty locations
  • NOT where temperature exceeds 40¬∞C

Cost ¬£20-40 + installation ¬£10-25
Reliability More reliable (backup battery) Depends on battery changes
Installation Electrician required DIY (screws/adhesive)

Testing Requirements

Landlord Obligations:

  • Before each new tenancy: Test all alarms on day tenant moves in
  • Document the test: Record in inventory/move-in checklist that alarms tested and working
  • Repairs: Responsible for repairing/replacing faulty alarms
  • Between tenancies: Test and replace as needed

Tenant Obligations:

  • Weekly testing: Tenants should test alarms weekly (press test button)
  • Report faults: Must inform landlord if alarm not working
  • Battery replacement: Tenants may be responsible for battery changes (clarify in AST)
  • Don't disable: Tenants must not remove batteries or disable alarms

How to Test:

  • Press and hold test button for 5-10 seconds
  • Alarm should sound loudly (85 decibels minimum)
  • If no sound or weak sound, replace battery or unit
  • CO alarm: some have test/reset button, check manufacturer instructions

Maintenance Schedule

Monthly (Tenant Responsibility):

  • Test all alarms using test button
  • Report any issues to landlord immediately

Every 6 Months (Landlord):

  • Check batteries in battery-powered alarms
  • Vacuum/dust alarm sensors (dust reduces effectiveness)
  • Check for damage or loose fittings

Annually (Landlord):

  • Replace backup batteries in mains-powered alarms
  • Test all alarms during quarterly inspection
  • Check manufacturing date (replace if >10 years old)

Every 10 Years:

  • Replace all smoke alarms (sensors degrade)
  • CO alarms typically 5-7 year lifespan (check manufacturer guidance)

When Alarms Activate

Smoke Alarm Sounds:

  • Check for fire - if present, evacuate and call 999
  • If false alarm from cooking/steam, ventilate area
  • Reset alarm once smoke clears
  • If persistent false alarms, relocate alarm away from kitchen/bathroom

CO Alarm Sounds:

  • Immediate action: Evacuate property, open windows/doors
  • Call emergency: Gas Emergency 0800 111 999
  • Do not re-enter: Until gas engineer confirms safe
  • Do not: Use electrical switches, mobile phones inside (can ignite gas)
  • Medical attention: CO poisoning symptoms = headache, dizziness, nausea - seek help

Penalties for Non-Compliance

  • Local authority fine: Up to ¬£5,000 per property
  • Enforcement action: Remedial notice requiring installation within specified time
  • Criminal prosecution: If failure results in death/serious injury
  • Insurance: Claims may be rejected if alarms not fitted
  • Civil liability: Tenant injury claims if alarms missing/faulty

‚ö†Ô∏è Section 21 Blocker

What is the How to Rent Guide?

The How to Rent guide is a government-published booklet explaining tenants' rights and responsibilities. It covers:

  • Understanding tenancy agreements
  • Deposits and how they're protected
  • Tenant rights during tenancy
  • What happens if things go wrong

  • All new assured shorthold tenancies
  • Every tenant (if multiple tenants, each should receive)
  • Required for new tenancies AND renewals (if version has updated)

Consequences of Not Providing:

  • Cannot serve valid Section 21: Major issue if need to regain possession
  • Can be remedied by providing guide later, but delays possession
  • Local authority can impose financial penalties
  • Breach of legal obligation even if no penalty applied

Renters Rights Bill 2025

Upcoming changes to rental law and how to prepare.

üì¢ Major Reform Coming

The Renters (Reform) Bill represents the biggest change to rental law in decades. Expected to pass in 2025, it will fundamentally change how landlords operate. Start preparing now.

Current Status (February 2025)

  • Bill status: Currently going through Parliament
  • Expected royal assent: Mid-2025
  • Implementation: Phased rollout likely - 6-12 months after royal assent
  • This page updated: February 2025 (check gov.uk for latest)
‚ö†Ô∏è Monitor for Updates

Bill details may change during parliamentary process. Subscribe to NRLA updates or check gov.uk regularly for final confirmed changes.

Major Changes Overview

What's Changing:
  1. Section 21 "no-fault" evictions abolished
  2. All tenancies become periodic from day one
  3. Expanded Section 8 grounds for possession
  4. Landlord must join redress scheme (Ombudsman)
  5. Private Rented Sector Database (registration required)
  6. Awaab's Law extended to private sector (hazard response times)
  7. Decent Homes Standard applied to private rentals
  8. Stronger tenant rights (pets, personalisation, challenging rent increases)
  9. Ban on rental bidding wars
  10. Tighter rules on rent increases

Key Changes in Detail

1. Section 21 Abolition

‚ö†Ô∏è Biggest Change: No More No-Fault Evictions

Current: Can serve Section 21 after fixed term ends, tenant must leave (no reason needed)

New: Section 21 abolished completely. Can only regain possession using Section 8 with specific grounds.

What This Means:

  • Must have valid reason to evict tenant
  • Cannot end tenancy just because you "want property back"
  • More difficult to remove problematic tenants
  • Tenant referencing becomes critical
  • Need to be more selective about who you rent to

New Grounds for Possession (Expanded):

  • Landlord moving in: Must give 2 months notice, can't re-let for 3 months
  • Selling property: Must give 2 months notice, must genuinely intend to sell
  • Repeated rent arrears: 3 instances of late payment in 3 years
  • Anti-social behavior: Proven ASB
  • Breach of tenancy: Substantial breach of agreement
  • 2+ months rent arrears: Mandatory ground (as now)

Preparation Steps:

  • Improve tenant referencing process
  • Keep detailed records of any issues
  • Act quickly on rent arrears (more important than ever)
  • Understand new possession grounds thoroughly
  • Consider whether you want to continue as landlord

2. Periodic Tenancies from Day One

Current: Typically 6-12 month fixed term, then periodic

New: All tenancies periodic from start (rolling month-to-month)

Impact:

  • Tenants can leave with 2 months notice at any time
  • Less certainty over rental income
  • More tenant turnover possible
  • Can't use fixed term to secure long-term tenant

Mitigation:

  • Good tenant relationships more important than ever
  • Maintain property well to encourage retention
  • Competitive rent to retain good tenants
  • Consider incentives for long-term tenants

3. Landlord Redress Scheme (Ombudsman)

New requirement: All landlords must join government-approved redress scheme

What It Is:

  • Independent ombudsman to resolve landlord-tenant disputes
  • Free for tenants to use
  • Binding decisions on landlords
  • Covers complaints about: repairs, deposits, behavior, charges

Requirements:

  • Cost: Expected ¬£100-200/year membership
  • Mandatory: Cannot let without membership
  • Penalty: Up to ¬£5,000 fine for not joining
  • Details in tenancy agreement: Must inform tenants of ombudsman membership

Approved Schemes:

  • Property Redress Scheme
  • Property Ombudsman
  • Others to be confirmed when law passes

Preparation:

  • Research available schemes now
  • Budget ¬£100-200/year in costs
  • Ensure excellent record keeping (for disputes)
  • Join when requirement becomes active

4. Private Rented Sector Database

New requirement: Register all rental properties on government database

Purpose:

  • Create public register of all rental properties
  • Track which properties are rented and by whom
  • Enforcement of standards
  • Identify rogue landlords

What You'll Need to Provide:

  • Property address
  • Landlord details
  • Compliance certificates (Gas, EICR, EPC)
  • Deposit protection details
  • Tenancy details
  • Redress scheme membership

Expected Details:

  • Cost: Unknown (possibly free or small annual fee)
  • Renewal: Likely annual registration
  • Penalty: Cannot let without registration
  • Portal: Online registration system

Preparation:

  • Ensure all compliance up to date
  • Keep digital copies of all certificates
  • Maintain accurate tenant records
  • Register as soon as system launches

5. Awaab's Law (Hazard Response Times)

Background: Named after toddler Awaab Ishak who died from mold exposure in social housing

New Requirements:

  • Category 1 hazards: Investigate within 14 days, fix within reasonable time
  • Damp and mold: Investigate within 14 days, fix within reasonable time (case-specific)
  • Emergency repairs: 24-hour response for genuine emergencies
  • Tenant reporting: Must provide clear reporting mechanism

Category 1 Hazards Include:

  • Damp and mould growth
  • Excess cold
  • Falls (stairs, steps, uneven surfaces)
  • Fire hazards
  • Hot surfaces and materials
  • Electrical hazards
  • Gas leaks

Preparation:

  • Establish clear repair reporting system
  • Build relationships with reliable contractors
  • Respond quickly to tenant repair requests
  • Keep records of all repair requests and responses
  • Conduct preventative maintenance (reduce emergencies)
  • Consider damp/mold prevention measures in old properties

6. Decent Homes Standard

Currently: Applies to social housing only

Proposed: Extended to all private rentals

Decent Homes Standard Requires:

  • Free from Category 1 hazards: Serious health and safety risks addressed
  • Reasonable state of repair: Key building components (roof, walls, windows, heating) in good condition
  • Modern facilities: Reasonably modern kitchen (< 20 years), bathroom (< 30 years)
  • Adequate heating: Efficient heating system, adequate insulation

Impact on Old Terraced Properties:

  • May require significant investment
  • Victorian/Edwardian properties often need: insulation improvements, new heating systems, kitchen/bathroom upgrades
  • Could make some properties uneconomical to let

Preparation:

  • Assess current property condition against standards
  • Budget for potential upgrades
  • Prioritize improvements to worst areas
  • Consider energy efficiency improvements (EPC rating)
  • May need to increase rents to cover costs

7. Tenant Rights Expansion

Right to Request Pets:

  • Cannot blanket ban pets
  • Tenant can request to keep pet
  • Must have reasonable grounds to refuse
  • Can require pet damage insurance
  • Can increase deposit (within limits) for pets

Right to Personalise:

  • Tenants can make reasonable alterations (decorating, hanging pictures)
  • Landlord can set conditions
  • Must return to original at end of tenancy (if required)

Challenge Rent Increases:

  • Rent increases only once per year (as now)
  • Tenant can challenge "unreasonable" increases at tribunal
  • Market rent will be determined by tribunal
  • Landlords must justify increases

Preparation:

  • Develop pet policy (criteria for acceptance)
  • Consider pet-friendly approach (expands tenant pool)
  • Set clear decoration/alteration guidelines
  • Research market rents carefully before increasing
  • Be prepared to justify rent increases

Key Changes Overview

Section 21 Abolition

üö´ End of No-Fault Evictions

What's Changing:

  • Section 21 notices will no longer exist
  • Cannot evict without proving specific grounds
  • All possession claims must use Section 8 with valid grounds
  • Periodic tenancies only - no more fixed terms (becomes periodic automatically)

New/Expanded Grounds for Possession:

  • Landlord moving in: Can evict with 2 months notice (once per 3 years)
  • Selling property: Can evict with 2 months notice
  • Rent arrears: Existing grounds remain (mandatory if 2+ months)
  • Anti-social behavior: Strengthened grounds
  • Repeated breach: Can evict for repeated breaches of tenancy
  • Student accommodation: Special provisions for purpose-built student housing

What This Means for You:

  • Can't just end tenancy: Need valid reason
  • Tenant vetting more critical: Harder to remove problem tenants
  • Must prove grounds: Need evidence for all possession claims
  • Longer possession timelines: Every case goes to court, judge discretion
  • Document everything: Need evidence trail for ASB, breaches, etc.

Before Section 21 abolition: If you have problematic tenants, consider serving Section 21 NOW while still available. Once abolished, removing difficult tenants becomes much harder.

Awaab's Law (Hazard Response Times)

‚è∞ Strict Repair Timelines

Background:

Named after Awaab Ishak, 2-year-old who died from mould exposure in social housing. Law now extending to private rentals.

Key Requirements:

  • Serious hazards: Must investigate within 14 days
  • Emergency repairs: Within 24 hours
  • Mould and damp: Investigate within 14 days, start remedial work within reasonable time
  • All repairs: Must complete within "reasonable time" (to be defined)
  • Communication: Must keep tenant informed of progress

Hazard Categories:

  • Category 1 (serious): Immediate risk to health/safety
  • Category 2 (less serious): Risk but not immediate
  • Examples: Damp/mould, electrical hazards, fire safety, structural issues

Penalties:

  • Fines for failing to meet timelines
  • Rent Repayment Orders
  • Enforcement action by council
  • Reputational damage if becoming letting agent

What to Do:

  • Set up system for logging and tracking repair requests
  • Respond to all repair reports within 24-48 hours
  • For serious issues (damp/mould), inspect within 14 days
  • Have contractor relationships ready for quick response
  • Document all communications and actions taken
  • Don't ignore tenant complaints - they now have legal backing

Decent Homes Standard

üè† Minimum Property Standards

What It Is:

Standard currently applied to social housing, being extended to private rentals. Sets minimum requirements for property condition.

Key Criteria (Proposed):

  • In reasonable repair
  • Reasonably modern facilities and services
  • Adequate heating and insulation
  • Free from Category 1 hazards (serious safety issues)

Specifics May Include:

  • Kitchen fitted in last 20 years (or satisfactory condition)
  • Bathroom fitted in last 30 years (or satisfactory condition)
  • Adequate heating (heating in all rooms, efficient system)
  • Adequate thermal comfort (insulation to modern standards)
  • Windows in good repair (no excessive draughts)

Impact on Old Terraced Properties:

  • Victorian/Edwardian properties may need upgrading
  • Single-glazed sash windows may not meet standards
  • Old boilers may need replacing
  • Loft and wall insulation may be required
  • Kitchens/bathrooms may need updating

Timeline:

  • Full details still being finalized
  • Likely phased implementation (new tenancies first, then existing)
  • Grace period expected for compliance

What to Do:

  • Assess your properties against likely standards
  • Budget for potential upgrades (¬£2,000-10,000+ per property estimated)
  • Priority: heating, insulation, damp issues
  • Consider phased improvements
  • Watch for final guidance when published

Other Key Changes

Rent Increases

  • Limited to once per year (already largely in force)
  • Tenant can challenge via tribunal if unreasonable
  • Must give 2 months notice of increase
  • Increases must reflect market rate, not punitive

Pets

  • Cannot blanket ban pets
  • Must have reasonable grounds to refuse
  • Can request pet insurance
  • Cannot charge pet deposit (pet damage insurance instead)

Bidding Wars

  • Cannot encourage or accept rent offers above asking price
  • Must advertise rent at rate you'll accept
  • Breach = fine up to ¬£7,000

Moving Out Fees

  • Tenant Fees Act already limits fees
  • Will be strengthened - only rent, deposit, holding deposit allowed
  • Lost keys at actual replacement cost only
  • No admin fees, reference fees, check-out fees
